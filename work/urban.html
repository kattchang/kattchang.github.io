URBAN MENAGERIE
An app and guide to safely interacting with animals in urban settings.


CHALLENGE
We wanted to see if people knew what to do in situations where an animal is lost, injured or deceased. As I discussed the situation with designers Vivian Lee and Darren Law, we noted most people didn't have clear knowledge in helping these creatures. It also appeared that there weren't many immediate resources. Moreover, when various websites did have information, they differed slightly in instructions which could cause confusion. Due to this variance, it would be time-consuming to look up the websites/resources in times of emergencies. This could deter the individual from helping the animal, or even lead to injury of the person or animal. To tackle this problem, my group and I came up with the idea of making an mobile app. This would reduce the time for searching resources and would also serve to inform the public on ways to co-exist with the animals that share their homes.

PROCESS
In order to execute our idea, we needed to gather information on what the public knew. We created an online survey to ask people in our community to fill it out, and consulted professionals like the Yolo County Biologist for further information. Wireframing was our next steps to figure out the best way to organize our functions and data. Two main ways we thought of were either categorizing by the situation first or the identification of the species first.



With our wireframing in place, we set out to do a Card Sort (method) with our fellow classmates and professors to find the best way to sort our app links. Many people sorted the cards by doing Found, Lost, Injured first then putting the categories of different animals.



BRANDING
As an individual role, I lead the branding and came up with the logo graphic that depicts a fox tail. Upon accident, we found that the name "Urban Menagerie" had a nice acronym, "UM", that went well with the logo graphic. Since "um" is a sound people might make before asking a question, we came to an agreement to put these elements together. By doing so, the logo represented the confusion and questions our users would ask if they found an animal.

FEATURES
This app is organized by LOST, INJURED, DEAD, and I DON'T KNOW. The categorization was decided because we realized that having all the information on one page was overwhelming to the user. Instead, with this method we were able to separate key information the user should take away immediately.

When an user clicks on "an injured animal?" it'll bring them to another page with animal categories for the user to choose from. Once chosen, the animal description page will pop up (Mallard page shown below) and show detailed information. This page went through several interations before we designed a more user-friendly interface. This layout also incororated all the information better, with icons emphasizing each sections. The information on this page is also accompanied by the call button, "CALL CDFW". This allows the user to dial the California Department of Fish and Wildlife for immediate help.

FINAL THOUGHTS
It was hard to condense the information we wanted to show in an app. Right now, there's still a lot of content and jargon that needs to be cut down. Our next steps would be to rearrange some of the content's hierarchy to further improve the user interface. On top of that, incorporating more resources and data would definitely help in bringing the user to a more accurate contact during specific situations.
